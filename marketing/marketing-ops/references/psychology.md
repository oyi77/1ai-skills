# Marketing Psychology & Mental Models Reference

Apply behavioral science to marketing ethically. These aren't tricks — they're
understanding how humans actually make decisions, so you can reduce friction
and communicate value more clearly.

## Ethical Framework

Before applying any principle, pass the **Transparency Test**:
"If I fully explained this technique to my customer, would they thank me for it?"
- YES → Persuasion (helping them decide). Proceed.
- NO → Manipulation (exploiting them). Stop.

## Decision Architecture Models

### Anchoring
The first number seen heavily influences subsequent judgments.
- **Pricing:** Show highest plan first → mid-tier feels reasonable
- **Discounts:** Show original price crossed out → discount feels significant
- **Proposals:** Open with ambitious scope → negotiated scope still generous

### Loss Aversion
Losses feel ~2× as painful as equivalent gains. People act harder to avoid loss.
- "Don't lose your progress" > "Keep building"
- "Your trial expires in 3 days" > "You have 3 days left"
- Money-back guarantees work because loss of money feels worse than gain of product

### Status Quo Bias
People prefer the current state. Change requires effort and feels risky.
- Make your product the path of least resistance (pre-selected plans, smart defaults)
- Frame switching cost as low: "Set up in 5 minutes, import your data in one click"
- Pre-fill forms, use smart defaults, reduce decisions required

### Choice Overload (Hick's Law)
More options → more friction → fewer decisions. 3 pricing tiers > 7.
- Reduce choices to 3-4 maximum per decision point
- Highlight "most popular" or "recommended" to guide choice
- Use progressive disclosure: show basics first, details on demand

### Decoy Effect
Adding an asymmetrically dominated option makes the target option look better.
- Three pricing plans where the middle one is clearly the best value
- "Pro" plan that's only slightly more than "Basic" but way more features

## Social Proof Models

### Bandwagon Effect
People follow what others are doing. Popularity signals quality and safety.
- User counts: "Join 50,000+ teams"
- Logo walls: trusted company names
- Testimonials with specific results, real names, photos
- "Trending" and "Popular" labels
- Review scores and star ratings

### Authority
People trust experts and credible sources.
- Industry certifications, awards, media mentions
- Expert endorsements and advisor boards
- "As seen in" press logos
- Author credentials on content
- Data-backed claims with citations

### Social Identity
People adopt behaviors consistent with groups they identify with.
- "Built for founders" vs "Built for everyone"
- Use language and imagery reflecting target identity
- Community belonging: "Join the movement"

## Pricing Psychology

### Charm Pricing
$49 feels significantly cheaper than $50 because the left digit dominates.
- Use for products under $100
- For premium positioning, use round numbers ($100, $500)

### Rule of 100
- Under $100 → percentage discounts feel bigger ("20% off")
- Over $100 → absolute discounts feel bigger ("$50 off")

### Mental Accounting
People treat money differently based on its source or category.
- "$1/day" feels cheaper than "$365/year" (different mental account)
- "Less than your morning coffee" → trivializes the cost
- Separate "savings" from "cost" in messaging

### Pain of Paying
Every payment triggers a micro-pain. Reduce payment friction.
- Free trial before payment (delay pain)
- Annual billing (one pain vs twelve)
- "Pay once, use forever" messaging
- Round numbers feel more like a "fair deal"

### Price Anchoring on Pages
```
Plan order on pricing page:
1. Enterprise: $499/mo (anchor — makes everything else feel cheap)
2. Professional: $99/mo ← "Most Popular" badge (target plan)
3. Starter: $29/mo (for price-sensitive buyers)
```

## Behavioral Triggers

### Scarcity
Limited availability increases perceived value and urgency.
- ✅ "Only 3 spots left in this cohort" (if true)
- ✅ "Early bird pricing ends Friday" (real deadline)
- ❌ "Only 2 left!" (fake scarcity destroys trust)
- Only use genuine scarcity. Fabricated urgency is manipulation.

### Reciprocity
People feel obligated to return favors. Give first.
- Free content, free tools, free tiers → builds obligation
- "Here's a free audit of your site" → they're more likely to listen to your pitch
- Generous free trials → users feel invested

### Commitment & Consistency
Once people commit to something small, they want to stay consistent.
- Micro-commitments: "Sign up for free" → "Complete your profile" → "Upgrade"
- "You've already invested 15 minutes setting this up..." (sunk cost + commitment)
- Progress bars and checklists leverage this powerfully

### Endowment Effect
People value things more once they feel ownership.
- Free trials make users feel they "own" the product → harder to give up
- "Your dashboard," "Your workspace" (possessive language)
- Customization during trial → invests effort → increases switching cost

### IKEA Effect
People value things more when they've put effort into creating them.
- Let users customize, configure, build within your product
- Onboarding that involves creation (not just reading) increases retention
- Templates users can modify > pre-built solutions

## Communication Models

### Framing Effect
How you say something matters as much as what you say.
- "95% uptime" vs "Only 5% downtime" (same fact, different feeling)
- "Save 10 hours per week" vs "Don't waste 10 hours per week"
- Positive frames for benefits, negative frames for risk/urgency

### Processing Fluency
Easy-to-process information feels more true and trustworthy.
- Simple language > complex jargon
- Clean design > cluttered pages
- Rhyming slogans remembered better ("Waze saves days")
- Short sentences, common words, clear hierarchy

### Peak-End Rule
People judge experiences by the peak moment and the ending, not the average.
- Make signup/checkout celebration delightful (confetti, congratulations)
- Nail the first-use experience (the "peak" of onboarding)
- End interactions positively (thank you pages, confirmation emails)

### Zeigarnik Effect
People remember incomplete tasks better than completed ones.
- Progress bars motivate completion ("You're 80% there!")
- "You have 2 steps remaining" emails drive activation
- Incomplete profiles create return visits

## Application by Funnel Stage

### TOFU (Awareness)
Primary models: Authority, Social Proof, Reciprocity
- Give valuable content freely (reciprocity)
- Use expert endorsements and data (authority)
- Show community size and engagement (social proof)

### MOFU (Consideration)
Primary models: Framing, Anchoring, Commitment
- Frame comparison in your favor (framing)
- Anchor against competitors or old way of doing things (anchoring)
- Get micro-commitments: email signup, free trial, quiz (commitment)

### BOFU (Decision)
Primary models: Loss Aversion, Scarcity, Endowment
- "Don't lose your free trial data" (loss aversion)
- "Offer expires" only if genuinely time-limited (scarcity)
- "Keep your customized workspace" (endowment)

### Retention
Primary models: IKEA Effect, Commitment, Status Quo Bias
- Deepen investment through customization (IKEA)
- Celebrate milestones and usage streaks (commitment)
- Make the product the default tool (status quo)

## Ethical Guardrails

Always apply these checks before using psychological principles:

1. **Is it truthful?** Never fabricate scarcity, reviews, or data
2. **Does it help the customer?** Persuasion helps them decide. Manipulation exploits
3. **Would you feel good explaining it?** If you'd hide it, don't do it
4. **Is the product genuinely good?** Psychology can't fix a bad product
5. **Are you creating value?** Good marketing makes buying easier, not trickier
