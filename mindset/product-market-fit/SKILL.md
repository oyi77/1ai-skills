# Product-Market Fit

Framework for finding and measuring product-market fit (PMF). Covers the Superhuman PMF survey, Sean Ellis test, retention cohorts, the Mom Test, and iteration strategies.

## When to Use

- Pre-PMF: validating a new product
- Measuring PMF quantitatively (NPS, retention, growth)
- Deciding whether to pivot or persevere
- Evaluating growth trajectory and market signals
- **When NOT to use**: Post-PMF scaling (focus on growth and distribution), or early ideation before talking to customers

## What Is PMF?

**Definition**: Product-Market Fit = You've built a product that a real market needs badly enough to pay for, use, and tell others about.

**Marc Andreessen**: "The market pulls the product out of the startup."

**Signs of PMF**:
- Users can't imagine going back to life before your product
- Organic growth (word of mouth)
- Usage grows without marketing push
- Users are disappointed when product is down

## PMF Survey (Sean Ellis)

**Question**: "How would you feel if you could no longer use the product?"

| Answer | Score | Meaning |
|--------|-------|---------|
| Very disappointed | 40%+ | Strong PMF indicator |
| Somewhat disappointed | 20-40% | Some PMF, needs work |
| Not disappointed | <20% | No PMF yet |

**Result**: If ≥40% say "Very disappointed" → PMF found.

**Example**: Superhuman achieved 58% (best-in-class).

**How to run**:
1. Survey active users (who've used product 2+ times)
2. Target N = 100+ responses for statistical significance
3. Track answer distribution
4. Segment by cohort (what do "very disappointed" users have in common?)

## NPS (Net Promoter Score)

**Question**: "How likely are you to recommend us to a friend? (0-10)"

**Scoring**:
- **9-10**: Promoters (love the product)
- **7-8**: Passives (satisfied but not enthusiastic)
- **0-6**: Detractors (unhappy)

**Formula**: `NPS = % Promoters - % Detractors`

**Range**: -100 to +100

**Benchmarks**:
- **50+**: Excellent (strong PMF indicator)
- **30-50**: Good (some PMF, room to improve)
- **<30**: Needs work (weak PMF)
- **<0**: Danger zone (product isn't working)

**Example**: Tesla NPS = 97 (best in auto). Facebook NPS = -3 (worst in tech).

## Retention Cohort Analysis

**Principle**: PMF = users retain (come back and stay).

**Retention curve shapes**:

| Shape | Meaning | PMF Status |
|-------|---------|------------|
| **Flatline** | Users keep coming back | ✅ Strong PMF |
| **Gradual decline** | Users churn slowly | ⚠️ Weak PMF (improve stickiness) |
| **Plummet** | Users leave quickly | ❌ No PMF (critical issue) |

**Example**:
- **Flatline**: Slack (30% DAU/MAU, even after years)
- **Plummet**: Most failed social apps (try once, never return)

**How to measure**:
1. Define "active" (e.g., logged in, used core action)
2. Track retention by cohort (users who joined Week 1, Week 2, etc.)
3. Graph retention rate over time (Day 1, Day 7, Day 30, Day 90)
4. If curve flattens → PMF. If keeps going down → no PMF.

## The Mom Test (Rob Fitzpatrick)

**Principle**: Talk to customers honestly. Avoid biased feedback.

### Bad Questions (that get nice lies)

| Question | Problem |
|----------|---------|
| "Would you use this product?" | Everyone says yes |
| "Do you like this feature?" | Same—people are polite |
| "How much would you pay?" | They'll say less than they actually pay |

### Good Questions

| Question | What you learn |
|----------|----------------|
| "Tell me about the last time you had this problem." | Real behavior, not hypothetical |
| "What have you tried to solve it?" | Current solution (competitors, DIY, doing nothing) |
| "Would you pay $X for a solution that does Y?" | Price sensitivity + real interest |
| "What would you do if I took it away?" | Real value (hard to fake) |

### Conversation Structure

1. **Frame**: "I'm trying to understand [problem]. Can I ask about your experience?"
2. **Explore**: Ask about past behavior (not hypothetical)
3. **Deep dive**: "What happened then? How did that feel?"
4. **Commitment**: "Would you be open to a demo next week?" (sign of real interest)

## Iteration Strategies

### If PMF Score < 40%

| Issue | Diagnosis | Solution |
|-------|-----------|----------|
| Problem isn't painful enough | Users don't care | Find a worse problem |
| Wrong segment | Right product, wrong market | Find a segment that cares more |
| Wrong solution | Users need different thing | Pivot product (different features) |
| Usability issues | Hard to use | Simplify onboarding, improve UX |

**Iterate on**:
1. **Problem**: Are we solving the right problem? (talk to more customers)
2. **Segment**: Is this the right customer? (try adjacent segment)
3. **Solution**: Is our product the right solution? (pivot features)
4. **Distribution**: Are we reaching them? (try new channels)

---

### If PMF Score ≥ 40%

| Action | Why |
|--------|-----|
| **Double down on what works** | Focus on the segment that gives you PMF |
| **Optimize onboarding** | Make "aha moment" faster |
| **Invest in growth** | Expand reach (content, paid, referrals) |
| **Hire** | Scale the team (support, sales, eng) |

## PMF Scorecard

| Metric | Target | Your Score |
|--------|--------|------------|
| "Very disappointed" if product gone | ≥40% | |
| NPS | ≥50 | |
| Retention (D30) | ≥30% of D1 active | |
| Organic growth rate | ≥5% month over month | |
| Time to "aha moment" | <5 min (or as short as possible) | |

**Pass**: 3/5 metrics met → PMF.

## Common Rationalizations

| Rationalization | Reality |
|-----------------|---------|
| "Everyone I talk to likes it" | They're being polite. Ask for money or commitment to reveal truth. |
| "We have some users" | Do they come back? Do they pay? Do they refer? |
| "We'll find PMF eventually" | PMF is binary. You either have it or you don't. Improve or pivot. |
| "We don't need to measure it" | If you can't measure PMF, you're guessing. Guess wrong → waste years. |

## Red Flags

- You haven't run a PMF survey (no quantitative signal)
- Retention curve keeps dropping (users don't come back)
- NPS < 30 (users aren't enthusiastic)
- No organic growth (every new user is paid acquisition)
- Customer churn >5%/month (they leave as fast as they arrive)

## Verification

- [ ] PMF survey sent (Sean Ellis question: "How would you feel if gone?")
- [ ] NPS measured (promoters % - detractors %)
- [ ] Retention cohort analysis run (D1, D7, D30, D90 retention curve)
- [ ] "Mom Test" conversations done (asked about past behavior, not hypothetical)
- [ ] PMF scorecard calculated (3/5 metrics met = PMF)
- [ ] If PMF < 40%: pivot plan documented (problem, segment, solution, distribution hypotheses)
- [ ] If PMF ≥ 40%: growth plan defined (onboarding, distribution, hiring)
