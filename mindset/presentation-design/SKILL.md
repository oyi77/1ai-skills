# Presentation Design

Framework for creating persuasive, memorable presentations. Covers slide design, narrative flow, data visualization, and audience engagement.

## When to Use

- Building pitch decks, keynote presentations, or sales decks
- Designing internal presentations for executives or teams
- Creating webinar or conference talk slides
- **When NOT to use**: Written reports, technical documentation, or dense reference materials

## Core Principles

### The 10/20/30 Rule (Guy Kawasaki)

**For pitch decks**:
- **10 slides**: Maximum for a pitch (forces focus)
- **20 minutes**: Present in 20 min (leaves time for Q&A)
- **30-point font**: Minimum font size (forces simplicity)

**The 10 Slides**:
1. Problem
2. Solution
3. Business model
4. Market size
5. Competition
6. Traction
7. Team
8. Financials
9. Use of funds
10. Closing (vision)

### Slide Anatomy

Every slide needs:

| Element | Purpose | Rule |
|---------|---------|------|
| **Headline** | One takeaway | Full sentence, not topic ("Revenue grew 3x" not "Revenue") |
| **Visual** | Support the headline | Chart, image, or diagram — never clip art |
| **Data** | Prove the point | 1-3 numbers max per slide |
| **White space** | Readability | 40%+ of slide should be empty |

**Bad Slide**:
```
Q2 Results
- Revenue: $1.2M
- Costs: $800K
- Profit: $400K
- Customer count: 1,200
- Churn: 3%
[Bullet points, no visual, too much data]
```

**Good Slide**:
```
Headline: "We hit profitability in Q2"
Visual: Simple bar chart (Revenue vs Costs)
Data: $1.2M revenue, $800K costs, $400K profit
[Clean, focused, one takeaway]
```

### Narrative Arc

Structure your deck like a story:

1. **Hook**: Start with the problem or a surprising fact
2. **Build tension**: Show why this problem matters (stakes, urgency)
3. **Climax**: Present your solution
4. **Resolution**: Show proof (traction, case studies)
5. **Call to action**: What do you want from the audience?

**Example** (SaaS pitch):
1. Hook: "40% of SaaS startups die because they can't collect payment."
2. Tension: "Stripe is complex. PayPal has high fees. Homegrown billing breaks at scale."
3. Climax: "We built billing infrastructure that scales from $0 to $100M ARR."
4. Resolution: "500 companies process $2B annually through our platform."
5. CTA: "We're raising a $5M Series A. Join us."

## Data Visualization

### Chart Selection

| Data Type | Best Chart | Avoid |
|-----------|------------|-------|
| Comparison | Bar chart | Pie chart (hard to compare) |
| Trend over time | Line chart | Table |
| Part-to-whole | Stacked bar or treemap | 3D pie chart |
| Distribution | Histogram | Scatter (unless showing correlation) |
| Correlation | Scatter plot | Line chart |

### Rules

- One chart per slide
- Label axes clearly
- Use color intentionally (highlight key data, not decoration)
- Remove gridlines and chart junk
- Make the trend obvious at a glance

**Example**:
- ❌ 3D pie chart with 8 slices and legend
- ✅ Horizontal bar chart with top 3 categories labeled

## Audience Engagement

### Opening Hooks

- **Question**: "How many of you have lost a deal because your demo broke?"
- **Surprising stat**: "The average SaaS company wastes $50K/year on unused software."
- **Story**: "Three months ago, our server crashed during a $1M demo..."
- **Bold claim**: "I'm going to show you how to cut your AWS bill in half."

### Handling Q&A

1. **Repeat the question**: Ensures everyone heard it
2. **Pause before answering**: Shows you're thinking, not defensive
3. **Bridge back**: "Great question. That ties into slide 7..." (if relevant)
4. **Admit ignorance**: "I don't know, but I'll find out and follow up" (better than guessing)
5. **Park tangents**: "That's a great topic for a deeper conversation afterward."

### Delivery Tips

- **Rule of 3**: Group ideas in threes (memorable, digestible)
- **Pause for emphasis**: Silence after key points creates impact
- **Move with purpose**: Don't pace aimlessly; plant your feet
- **Eye contact**: Look at individuals, not over heads
- **Energy**: Match energy to message (urgent problem = higher energy)

## Common Rationalizations

| Rationalization | Reality |
|-----------------|---------|
| "More slides = more impressive" | Brevity shows confidence. 10 tight slides > 50 rambling ones. |
| "Text on slides = I won't forget" | Slides are for the audience, not your notes. Use speaker notes. |
| "Animations make it engaging" | Excessive animations distract. Use sparingly (e.g., reveal data progressively). |
| "I need to cover everything" | Focus on what matters to *this* audience. Cut the rest. |

## Red Flags

- Slides are paragraphs of text (reading off slides)
- Font size < 24pt (audience can't read)
- More than 3 colors per slide (visual chaos)
- Clip art or cheesy stock photos (unprofessional)
- You apologize for the deck ("Sorry, lots of slides...")
- No clear call to action at the end

## Verification

- [ ] Slides follow 10/20/30 rule (or adapted version for your context)
- [ ] Every slide has one clear takeaway as the headline
- [ ] Data visualized (charts, not tables)
- [ ] Narrative arc: hook → tension → climax → resolution → CTA
- [ ] Rehearsed at least twice (under 20 min)
- [ ] Q&A prep: anticipated objections documented
