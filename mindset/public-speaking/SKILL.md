# Public Speaking

Framework for delivering engaging presentations: narrative structure, audience analysis, slide design, stage presence, and Q&A.

## When to Use

- Conference talks (20-45 min)
- Team presentations and all-hands
- Investor pitches and sales demos
- Internal strategy or update presentations
- Any presentation where audience engagement matters
- **When NOT to use**: Small team updates (use standup), or written updates (use email/doc)

## The 3-Act Structure

### 1. Setup (20% of time)

**Goal**: Hook the audience, set context, state the problem.

**Elements**:
- **Hook**: Surprising stat, story, question, or provocative statement
- **Context**: Why should they care?
- **Problem**: What's broken or missing?

**Example hook**: "In 2020, we almost shut down. Here's what we learned."

---

### 2. Body (60% of time)

**Goal**: Deliver the core content.

**Rule of 3**: Humans remember 3 things. Structure in 3 parts.

**Example** (talk about communication):
1. **Empathy**: Understand your audience
2. **Clarity**: Say it simply
3. **Structure**: Make it memorable

**Transition phrases**:
- "Now here's where it gets interesting..."
- "Let me give you a concrete example..."
- "But there's a catch..."

---

### 3. Close (20% of time)

**Goal**: Summarize, call to action, memorable closing.

**Elements**:
- **Summary**: Repeat the 3 things (they'll remember)
- **CTA**: "What I want you to do next"
- **Closing**: Full-circle back to the hook (bookend)

**Example close**: "Next time you present, remember: Empathy. Clarity. Structure. That's the difference between a talk and a transformation. [Back to hook story]"

## Audience Analysis

**Before the talk, ask**:

| Question | Why |
|----------|-----|
| Who is the audience? (role, seniority, domain) | Tailor examples and language |
| What do they already know? | Don't waste time on basics or talk over their heads |
| What do they care about? | Connect content to their priorities |
| What do they fear? | Address objections proactively |
| What do I want them to think/feel/do after? | Define success for this talk |

**For executives**: Bottom-line-up-front, decisions needed.
**For practitioners**: How-to, actionable takeaways.
**For mixed audiences**: High-level start, then optional depth.

## Slide Design

**Rule**: 1 idea per slide. Minimum text. Maximum visuals.

### Good slide:
```
[Image or diagram]
"Revenue grew 3x in 6 months"
```

### Bad slide (bullet heaven):
```
- Strategic transformation
  - This quarter we achieved...
  - Key initiatives include...
  - Q3 milestones...
- OKR alignment...
```

**Design principles**:
- **10% text rule**: If they read, they don't listen
- **Images > text**: Visuals stick (people remember 65% of visuals vs 10% of text)
- **Consistent format**: Same font, colors, layouts
- **Dark background**: Better for large rooms

**Deck structure**:
- Title slide (hook + your name)
- Problem (1-2 slides)
- Solution (3-5 slides, 1 idea each)
- Proof (1-2 slides, data + case study)
- Vision (1 slide)
- Call to action (1 slide)
- End slide (your name + contact)

## Stage Presence

### Body language

| Signal | Weak | Strong |
|--------|------|--------|
| **Posture** | Leaning, shifting weight | Grounded, open, shoulders back |
| **Hands** | In pockets, fidgeting | Gesturing naturally (above waist) |
| **Eye contact** | Reading slides, darting | Slow scan (3-5 sec per person) |
| **Movement** | Pacing nervously | Purposeful movement (change position between sections) |

### Vocal techniques

- **Pacing**: Slower for emphasis, faster for energy
- **Pauses**: 2-3 seconds after key points (gives time to land)
- **Volume**: Louder for impact, softer for intimacy
- **Variation**: Don't be monotone (vary pitch and pace)

### Manage nerves

- **Before**: Deep breaths (4 counts in, 6 counts out)
- **At start**: Memorize first 30 seconds (nerves fade after first minute)
- **During**: Focus on giving, not performing. "I'm sharing something valuable."
- **If you forget**: Pause. Breathe. Say "Let me reframe that." (audience doesn't know your script)

## Q&A Preparation

**Anticipate questions** (prepare 5-10 answers):

**Types**:
- **Clarification**: "Can you explain X more?"
- **Challenge**: "But what about Y?"
- **Edge case**: "What if the user is Z?"
- **Vision**: "Where do you see this going?"

**Handling tough questions**:
1. **Listen fully**: Don't interrupt
2. **Pause**: Count to 2 before answering (signals thoughtfulness)
3. **Acknowledge**: "Great question"
4. **Answer concisely**: 30-60 seconds
5. **Return to your message**: "And that's why [main point]"

**If you don't know**: "I don't have that information handy, but I'll follow up by email."

## Practice Framework

| Phase | Goal | Time |
|-------|------|------|
| **Outline** | Structure (hook, body, close) | 1-2 hours |
| **Slides** | Design (1 idea per slide) | 2-4 hours |
| **Stand-up practice** | Timing, transitions, delivery | 3-5 reps |
| **Peer feedback** | Blind spots, tough questions | 1-2 reps |
| **Final run-through** | Polish, confidence | 1 rep (day before) |

**Tips**:
- Time yourself (if 45 min slot, target 35 min talk + 10 min Q&A)
- Record yourself (watch for fillers, pacing, body language)
- Know your opening cold (reduces first-minute nerves)

## Common Rationalizations

| Rationalization | Reality |
|-----------------|---------|
| "I'm not a natural speaker" | Public speaking is a skill. Practice and feedback improve anyone. |
| "More slides = more content" | Fewer slides = more impact. 10 good slides > 50 bullet slides. |
| "I'll wing the Q&A" | Audiences remember Q&A most. Prepare for it. |
| "I need to cover everything" | Cover less, but deeply. 3 things remembered > 10 things forgotten. |

## Red Flags

- You read from slides (audience reads faster than you speak)
- You have more than 10 words per slide (text overload)
- You talk the whole time without audience interaction (monotone)
- You apologize ("Sorry, I'm nervous" / "This isn't my best slide")
- You end with "Any questions?" instead of a strong closing statement

## Verification

- [ ] 3-act structure used (hook, body with rule of 3, close with CTA)
- [ ] Audience analyzed (who, what they know, what they care about)
- [ ] Slides designed (1 idea per slide, minimal text, consistent format)
- [ ] Stage presence practiced (posture, gestures, eye contact, vocal variety)
- [ ] Tough questions anticipated and prepared (5+ prepared answers)
- [ ] Timing verified (talk + Q&A fits within slot)
- [ ] Peer feedback received and incorporated (1+ dry run)
