# First-Principles Thinking

Reasoning from fundamental truths rather than analogy or convention. Elon Musk's core problem-solving method: break down to physics, rebuild from scratch.

## When to Use

- Solving novel problems with no precedent
- Challenging industry assumptions or "best practices"
- Innovating in constrained environments (cost, time, resources)
- Questioning why things are done a certain way
- **When NOT to use**: Routine problems with proven solutions, tight deadlines requiring fast execution, or when reinventing the wheel wastes time

## The 5-Step Process

### 1. Identify the Problem

State it clearly. What are you actually trying to solve?

**Example**: "Electric cars are too expensive" → Real problem: "Battery packs cost $600/kWh"

### 2. Break Down to Fundamentals

Strip away assumptions. What are the physics/economics/facts?

**Analogy thinking** (lazy): "Battery packs have always cost $600/kWh, so they must cost that."

**First principles** (rigorous): "A battery pack is lithium, cobalt, nickel, polymer separators, and steel casing. What do those raw materials cost on commodity markets?"

### 3. Question Every Assumption

| Assumption | Challenge |
|------------|-----------|
| "We need a college degree for this role" | Do we need the degree or the skills it signals? Can we test skills directly? |
| "Customers want more features" | Do they want features or outcomes? Are we solving their job-to-be-done? |
| "This has always been done this way" | Why? What constraints existed then that don't exist now? |

**Framework**: Ask "Why?" 5 times to get past surface-level assumptions to root constraints.

### 4. Reason Up from Fundamentals

Rebuild the solution using only verified truths.

**Musk on batteries**:
- Raw materials cost ~$80/kWh
- Manufacturing/assembly adds cost
- "If we buy materials and manufacture ourselves, can we hit $100-150/kWh?"
- Result: Tesla built its own battery production, driving costs down 10x

### 5. Validate

Test your new model. Does it work? What did you miss?

## Common Assumptions to Challenge

| Domain | Conventional Wisdom | First-Principles Challenge |
|--------|---------------------|----------------------------|
| **Business** | "We need VC funding to scale" | Do we need capital or customers? Can we bootstrap and grow organically? |
| **Hiring** | "We need senior engineers" | Do we need experience or problem-solving ability? Can we train juniors? |
| **Marketing** | "We need a big launch campaign" | Do we need awareness or product-market fit first? Should we go narrow and deep? |
| **Pricing** | "Competitors charge $X, so we should too" | What's the value we deliver? What's our cost structure? Price from value, not competition. |

## Worked Example: Rocket Cost Reduction (SpaceX)

**Conventional wisdom**: "Rockets cost $60M per launch because aerospace is expensive."

**First principles breakdown**:
- What is a rocket? Aluminum, titanium, copper, carbon fiber, fuel.
- What do these materials cost? ~2% of the rocket's sale price.
- Why the 50x markup? Single-use rockets, government contracts, no competition, legacy aerospace bureaucracy.

**Rebuild**:
- Make rockets reusable (recover and relaunch)
- Manufacture in-house (vertical integration)
- Simplify design (fewer parts)

**Result**: SpaceX cut launch costs from $60M to $2M per flight.

## Common Rationalizations

| Rationalization | Reality |
|-----------------|---------|
| "That's just how it's done" | Conventions are optimized for past constraints. Context changes. |
| "Smart people already tried this" | Past failures often had different constraints (tech, capital, market). |
| "This is too complex to rethink" | Complexity often comes from accumulated assumptions. Strip them. |
| "We don't have time" | First-principles thinking saves time long-term by avoiding wrong paths. |

## Red Flags

- You're reasoning by analogy ("X works like Y, so...")
- You accept "industry standard" as justification
- You haven't challenged a single assumption in your plan
- You're intimidated by experts saying "it can't be done"
- You confuse convention with natural law
