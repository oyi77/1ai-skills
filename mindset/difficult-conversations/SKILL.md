# Difficult Conversations

Framework for high-stakes, emotionally charged conversations: critical feedback, disagreements, layoffs, performance issues. Based on *Crucial Conversations* and *Difficult Conversations* (Harvard Negotiation Project).

## When to Use

- Delivering critical feedback or performance reviews
- Addressing interpersonal conflict or disagreements
- Having termination, demotion, or discipline conversations
- Breaking bad news to stakeholders or clients
- **When NOT to use**: Casual feedback, low-stakes disagreements, or emergencies requiring immediate action

## Process

1. **Prepare**: Clarify your intent (what do you want for yourself, them, the relationship), rehearse with SBI/DESC
2. **Create safety**: Start with shared purpose, acknowledge stakes, invite dialogue
3. **Share your story**: Use SBI or DESC to describe facts + impact, avoid judgment
4. **Invite their story**: Ask for their perspective, listen without defending
5. **Co-create solutions**: Explore options together, commit to actions with follow-up

## Frameworks

### SBI Model (Situation-Behavior-Impact)

Concrete feedback without judgment.

**Template**:
- **Situation**: "In yesterday's standup..."
- **Behavior**: "...you interrupted Sarah twice while she was presenting."
- **Impact**: "It made her hesitant to share updates, and the team lost valuable context."

**Example**:
- ❌ "You're dismissive and rude." (Judgment)
- ✅ "In the client call Tuesday, when you said 'that's a non-issue,' the client went silent and ended the call early. We lost the upsell opportunity."

### DESC Script (Describe-Express-Specify-Consequences)

Structured script for high-stakes conversations.

1. **Describe**: Objective facts
2. **Express**: Your feelings/concerns
3. **Specify**: What you want to happen
4. **Consequences**: Positive (if they act) or negative (if they don't)

**Example**:
- **D**: "You've missed 3 of the last 5 sprint deadlines."
- **E**: "I'm concerned about team velocity and your workload."
- **S**: "I'd like you to flag blockers in standups and ask for help earlier."
- **C**: "If we do that, we can hit our Q2 target. If not, we'll need to reassign some of your work."

### 3 Conversations Framework

Every difficult conversation is really three conversations:

| Conversation | What It Is | How to Handle |
|--------------|------------|---------------|
| **What Happened?** | Disagreement on facts | Move from certainty to curiosity: "Here's what I saw. What did you see?" |
| **Feelings** | Emotional undercurrent | Name feelings explicitly: "I felt blindsided. How did you feel?" |
| **Identity** | Threat to self-image | Separate behavior from identity: "This mistake doesn't mean you're incompetent." |

## Common Rationalizations

| Rationalization | Reality |
|-----------------|---------|
| "They'll figure it out" | Avoiding feedback enables poor performance and erodes trust. |
| "I'll just send an email" | High-stakes conversations require synchronous dialogue. Email escalates. |
| "I need to soften this" | The feedback sandwich (compliment-criticism-compliment) confuses the message. Be direct and kind. |
| "They should know" | Unspoken expectations are unfair. Make expectations explicit. |

## Red Flags

- You're avoiding the conversation for >2 weeks
- You script what they'll say and argue with your imagined version
- You lead with "I'm not trying to criticize, but..." (undercuts your message)
- You use "always" or "never" ("You always miss deadlines")
- You deliver feedback in front of others (public criticism)
- You don't have specific examples (vague: "Your attitude is bad")

## Verification

- [ ] Specific examples prepared (Situation-Behavior-Impact)
- [ ] Your intent clarified (what do you want for them, not just yourself)
- [ ] Safety established at start (shared purpose, mutual respect)
- [ ] Their perspective heard and acknowledged
- [ ] Concrete next steps agreed upon with follow-up date
- [ ] Relationship intact or improved (not damaged)
