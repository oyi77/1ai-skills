# Scenario Planning

Framework for planning under uncertainty. Covers best/worst/likely case analysis, war-gaming, Monte Carlo thinking, and trigger-based pivots.

## When to Use

- High uncertainty about future outcomes (market, technology, regulation)
- Strategic decisions with long time horizons
- Risk management and contingency planning
- Testing robustness of strategies across scenarios
- **When NOT to use**: Low-uncertainty tactical decisions, emergencies requiring immediate action, or when only one plausible future exists

## The 3-Scenario Method

Build 3 scenarios: **Best Case**, **Worst Case**, **Most Likely**

### 1. Best Case

**What goes right?** Optimistic but plausible.

**Example** (SaaS startup launch):
- Viral growth (2x expected signups)
- Enterprise clients adopt quickly
- No major technical issues
- Competitor stumbles

**Metrics**: 10K users in 6 months, $500K ARR

### 2. Worst Case

**What goes wrong?** Pessimistic but plausible (not catastrophic outliers).

**Example**:
- Slow growth (50% of expected signups)
- Enterprise sales cycle longer than planned
- Security incident causes churn
- Competitor launches similar product

**Metrics**: 1K users in 6 months, $50K ARR

### 3. Most Likely

**Base case**: Blend of best/worst with realistic assumptions.

**Example**:
- Moderate growth (expected signups)
- Some enterprise traction, some delays
- Minor technical hiccups resolved quickly

**Metrics**: 5K users in 6 months, $200K ARR

## Scenario Planning Process

1. **Identify key uncertainties**: What factors drive outcomes? (customer adoption, competition, regulation, funding)
2. **Define plausible scenarios**: Best, worst, likely (or 4+ scenarios for complex situations)
3. **Quantify outcomes**: Metrics for each scenario (revenue, users, runway)
4. **Stress-test strategy**: Does your plan survive worst case? Does it capture best case upside?
5. **Set triggers**: What signals indicate which scenario is unfolding? Pivot rules.

## War-Gaming

**Purpose**: Simulate adversarial scenarios to test strategy.

**Process**:
1. Assign roles (e.g., your company, competitor A, competitor B, regulator)
2. Each team devises strategy to win
3. Play out 2-3 rounds of moves/countermoves
4. Debrief: What vulnerabilities emerged? What strategies worked?

**Example** (Market entry):
- **Your move**: Launch freemium product
- **Competitor A response**: Undercuts pricing
- **Your counter**: Focus on enterprise (higher margin)
- **Competitor B response**: Launches enterprise product
- **Insight**: Need differentiation beyond pricing

## Trigger-Based Pivots

**Principle**: Define in advance what conditions trigger a pivot.

| Scenario | Trigger Metric | Pivot Action |
|----------|----------------|--------------|
| **Best case** | 10K users by month 3 | Raise Series A, scale go-to-market |
| **Most likely** | 3-7K users by month 3 | Continue plan, monitor closely |
| **Worst case** | <2K users by month 3 | Pivot product, change ICP, or shut down |

**Why this matters**: Removes emotion from pivot decisions. Pre-commit to thresholds.

## Monte Carlo Thinking

**Concept**: Run many simulations with varied assumptions to understand distribution of outcomes.

**Example** (Revenue forecast):
- Assumption: Monthly growth rate = 10-30% (uniform distribution)
- Run 1000 simulations of 12-month growth
- **Result**: 80% confidence interval: $200K-$800K ARR

**Insight**: Wide range = high uncertainty. Plan for both ends.

## Common Rationalizations

| Rationalization | Reality |
|-----------------|---------|
| "Let's just plan for the most likely case" | Ignores tail risks and missed opportunities. Scenario planning hedges both. |
| "We can't predict the future" | True, but you can prepare for multiple futures. Scenarios ≠ predictions. |
| "Worst case is too depressing" | Ignoring it doesn't make it go away. Plan for it, then work to avoid it. |
| "This is too much work" | Scenario planning takes hours; pivoting without it takes months. |

## Red Flags

- You only plan for the "most likely" case (ignoring tails)
- Your "worst case" is unrealistically optimistic
- You don't set triggers for pivots (flying blind)
- You ignore early signals that a scenario is unfolding
- You treat scenarios as predictions (they're not—they're contingencies)

## Verification

- [ ] 3 scenarios defined (best, worst, likely) with quantified outcomes
- [ ] Key uncertainties identified (drivers of variance)
- [ ] Strategy stress-tested (survives worst case? Captures best case?)
- [ ] Triggers set for each scenario (when to pivot)
- [ ] War-gaming conducted (if adversarial dynamics matter)
- [ ] Team aligned on scenarios and pivot rules
