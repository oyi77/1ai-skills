# Conflict Resolution

Framework for resolving interpersonal and team conflicts: mediation, non-violent communication (NVC), de-escalation, and repair.

## When to Use

- Disagreements between team members
- Cross-team tension (blame, silos, misalignment)
- Customer or partner disputes
- Interpersonal friction in relationships
- **When NOT to use**: Systemic issues (process not people), or illegal/harassment situations (escalate to HR/legal)

## Conflict Types

| Type | Source | Example |
|------|--------|---------|
| **Task conflict** | Disagreement on approach | Which architecture to use? |
| **Process conflict** | How work gets done | Who owns what? |
| **Relationship conflict** | Personal friction | "I don't trust their motives" |

**Key**: Task and process conflict can be productive. Relationship conflict is always destructive.

---

## De-escalation First

**Before any resolution, de-escalate**:

1. **Pause**: Take 5 seconds before responding
2. **Breathe**: Deep breath (calms nervous system)
3. **Lower voice**: Speaks softer than normal (signals safety)
4. **Acknowledge emotion**: "I can see this is frustrating"
5. **Buy time**: "Let's take 5 minutes and come back"

**When to walk away**: If either party is shouting, name-calling, or threatening. Resume when calm.

---

## Non-Violent Communication (NVC)

**Structure**: Observations → Feelings → Needs → Requests

**Example**:

1. **Observation** (no judgment): "In the last 3 standups, you cut me off while I was giving my update."
2. **Feelings** (name emotion): "I feel dismissed and frustrated."
3. **Needs** (underlying need): "I need to feel heard and respected in our team updates."
4. **Request** (actionable, flexible): "Would you be willing to let me finish my update before interjecting?"

**Formula**:
> "When I see [observation], I feel [feeling] because I need [need]. Would you be willing to [request]?"

## Mediation Structure (for 3rd party)

**Setup**: Private, neutral location. Both parties agree to ground rules.

**Ground rules**:
- One person speaks at a time
- No interruptions
- Focus on facts, not accusations
- Goal is resolution, not winning

**Process**:

### Phase 1: Each person tells their story (5 min each)
- "What happened from your perspective?"
- "How did it affect you?"
- Interrupter says: "Let them finish. You'll get your turn."

### Phase 2: Identify interests (10 min)
- "What do you actually need here?"
- "What outcome would make this right?"
- Look for common ground (both want the project to succeed, both want respect)

### Phase 3: Generate options (10 min)
- "What solutions could work for both of you?"
- Brainstorm 3-5 options without judgment
- Narrow to 1 that both can accept

### Phase 4: Agreement (5 min)
- "Let's write down what we agreed on."
- Clear actions: "I will _____ (do X). You will _____ (do Y)."
- Follow-up: "Let's check in next week."

## Difficult Conversations Template

### Before the conversation

**Prepare**:
1. **Purpose**: What outcome do I want?
2. **Stakes**: What happens if we don't resolve this?
3. **Contribution**: What's my role in this conflict? (self-reflection)

**Structure**:

> "I'd like to talk about [topic]. It might be uncomfortable, but I think it's important for us to be honest with each other. Is now a good time?"

---

### Listen to understand

- Paraphrase: "So what I'm hearing is..."
- Validate: "That makes sense given what you've described."
- Ask questions: "Can you tell me more?"

---

### Speak from your perspective

- Use "I" statements, not "you" accusations
- "I felt disappointed when..." (not "You never follow through")
- "I need... " (not "You should...")

---

## Repair Attempts

**Principle**: After conflict, repair the relationship.

**Steps**:
1. **Acknowledge rupture**: "That conversation didn't go well."
2. **Take responsibility**: "I could have handled X better."
3. **Explain intent**: "My intention was Y, not what you experienced."
4. **Ask for feedback**: "How did it land for you?"
5. **Commit to change**: "Next time I'll do Z differently."

## Common Rationalizations

| Rationalization | Reality |
|-----------------|---------|
| "They started it. It's their fault." | Blame prevents resolution. Focus on your contribution. |
| "I'll just avoid them." | Avoidance escalates (resentment builds). Address it early. |
| "It's not professional to show emotion" | Emotion isn't the problem. How you handle it is. |
| "A good leader picks a side." | Mediation (third-party) is neutral. You serve the relationship, not one person. |

## Red Flags

- You avoid difficult conversations (tension compounds)
- You talk ABOUT them, not TO them (gossip, triangulation)
- You escalate to manager before trying resolution
- Conflict has become personal (not about tasks or process)
- One party feels unsafe or unheard

## Verification

- [ ] De-escalation used (pause, breathe, lower voice, acknowledge)
- [ ] NVC structure used (observation → feeling → need → request)
- [ ] If mediating: both parties told their stories without interruption
- [ ] Interests identified (underlying needs, not positions)
- [ ] Agreement documented (clear actions, follow-up scheduled)
- [ ] Repair attempt made (acknowledge rupture, take responsibility)
