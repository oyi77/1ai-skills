# Mental Models

Collection of thinking tools for better decision-making. 20+ models across reasoning, economics, and psychology.

## When to Use

- Analyzing complex decisions
- Avoiding cognitive biases
- Evaluating investments, strategies, or trade-offs
- **When NOT to use**: Overapplying models to simple decisions (analysis paralysis)

## Core Models

### 1. Circle of Competence

**Principle**: Know what you know (and don't know). Stay within your expertise or learn before acting.

**Application**: Before deciding, ask: "Do I understand this domain deeply?" If no, defer to experts or learn first.

**Example**: Buffett avoids tech stocks because they're outside his circle.

---

### 2. Inversion

**Principle**: Solve problems backward. Instead of "How do I succeed?", ask "How do I fail?" and avoid those paths.

**Application**: List all ways to fail, then ensure your plan avoids them.

**Example**: "How do I lose customers?" → Poor support, bugs, high prices. **Plan**: Prioritize support, quality, fair pricing.

---

### 3. Second-Order Thinking

**Principle**: Consider consequences of consequences. "And then what?"

**Application**: Map first-order (immediate) and second-order (downstream) effects before acting.

**Example**: Cut prices → more customers (1st order) → overload support → churn increases (2nd order).

---

### 4. Margin of Safety

**Principle**: Build buffers for uncertainty. Don't operate at 100% capacity.

**Application**: Estimate conservatively. Add 25-50% buffer to timelines, budgets, and capacity.

**Example**: Runway = 12 months → Operate as if it's 8 months (margin of safety).

---

### 5. Opportunity Cost

**Principle**: Choosing A means not choosing B. Evaluate what you're giving up.

**Application**: For every "yes," ask "What am I saying no to?"

**Example**: Building feature A (3 months) means not building feature B (which could have 2x ROI).

---

### 6. Compound Interest

**Principle**: Small, consistent gains compound exponentially over time.

**Application**: Favor strategies with compounding effects (skills, relationships, reputation, revenue).

**Example**: 1% daily improvement = 37x better in a year (1.01^365).

---

### 7. Network Effects

**Principle**: Value increases as more people use it.

**Application**: Design products/platforms where each user makes it better for others.

**Example**: Slack (more teammates = more valuable), Marketplace (more buyers attract sellers).

---

### 8. Economies of Scale

**Principle**: Unit cost decreases as volume increases.

**Application**: Identify where scale reduces costs (manufacturing, distribution, support).

**Example**: AWS is cheaper per instance than self-hosting at scale.

---

### 9. Sunk Cost Fallacy

**Principle**: Past costs are irrelevant to future decisions. Don't throw good money after bad.

**Application**: Ignore what you've already spent. Ask: "If I started today, would I make this decision?"

**Example**: "We've invested $1M in this product" → Irrelevant. Will the next $1M generate ROI?

---

### 10. Confirmation Bias

**Principle**: We seek evidence that confirms our beliefs and ignore contradictions.

**Application**: Actively seek disconfirming evidence. Ask: "What would prove me wrong?"

**Example**: "Our product is great" → Test: Run NPS survey, read negative reviews.

---

### 11. Occam's Razor

**Principle**: The simplest explanation is usually correct.

**Application**: Favor simple solutions over complex ones. Complexity hides errors.

**Example**: Bug could be race condition, memory leak, or cosmic ray → Start with simplest: bad input handling.

---

### 12. Hanlon's Razor

**Principle**: Don't attribute to malice what can be explained by stupidity (or ignorance).

**Application**: Assume good intent before assuming bad faith.

**Example**: Colleague missed deadline → Likely overloaded, not sabotaging you.

---

### 13. Lindy Effect

**Principle**: The longer something has survived, the longer it will likely survive.

**Application**: Favor old, proven tech/ideas over new, hyped ones.

**Example**: SQL databases (50 years) will outlive most NoSQL fads.

---

### 14. Pareto Principle (80/20)

**Principle**: 80% of outcomes come from 20% of inputs.

**Application**: Focus on the 20% that drives the most value.

**Example**: 20% of customers generate 80% of revenue → Focus on them.

---

### 15. Law of Diminishing Returns

**Principle**: Each additional unit of input yields less output.

**Application**: Know when more effort produces marginal gains.

**Example**: 90% test coverage = good. 99.9% = diminishing returns (effort > value).

---

### 16. Survivorship Bias

**Principle**: We only see winners, not the invisible losers.

**Application**: Don't assume success is typical. Account for failures you don't see.

**Example**: "Dropouts like Jobs succeeded" → Ignores 99% of dropouts who didn't.

---

### 17. Incentives (Munger's "Show me the incentives...")

**Principle**: People respond to incentives, often in unintended ways.

**Application**: Design incentives carefully. Ask: "What behavior does this reward?"

**Example**: Pay engineers per line of code → Bloated, unreadable code.

---

### 18. Asymmetric Risk/Reward

**Principle**: Favor bets where upside >> downside.

**Application**: Look for situations with capped downside, unlimited upside.

**Example**: Startup equity (lose initial investment, potential 100x return).

---

### 19. Via Negativa (Subtraction)

**Principle**: Improve by removing, not adding.

**Application**: Ask "What can I stop doing?" before "What should I add?"

**Example**: Faster product = Remove features, not optimize code.

---

### 20. Map ≠ Territory

**Principle**: Models are simplifications. Don't confuse the model with reality.

**Application**: Use models as guides, not gospel. Reality is messier.

**Example**: Financial models predict $1M ARR → Reality: customers behave unpredictably.

---

## How to Use

1. **Pattern match**: Does this situation fit a known model?
2. **Test**: Does the model suggest a non-obvious insight?
3. **Combine**: Use multiple models (e.g., Inversion + Second-Order Thinking)
4. **Avoid**: Don't force models where they don't fit (hammers ≠ everything is a nail)

## Verification

- [ ] Identified 2-3 relevant models for the decision
- [ ] Applied models to surface non-obvious insights
- [ ] Checked for cognitive biases (confirmation, sunk cost, survivorship)
- [ ] Combined models where appropriate (e.g., Inversion + Opportunity Cost)
- [ ] Didn't overfit (models are guides, not rules)
