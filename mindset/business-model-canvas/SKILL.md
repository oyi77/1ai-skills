# Business Model Canvas

Framework by Alexander Osterwalder for designing, analyzing, and iterating on business models. Covers all 9 building blocks with examples and validation.

## When to Use

- Launching a new product or venture
- Iterating on an existing business model
- Evaluating a competitor's strategy
- Pivoting or exploring new revenue streams
- **When NOT to use**: Deep financial modeling (use P&L), or early ideation without customer problem understanding

## The 9 Building Blocks

### 1. Customer Segments (Who?)

**Define**: The groups of people or organizations you aim to serve.

**Examples**:
- Mass market (e.g., Spotify: all music listeners)
- Niche market (e.g., Palantir: government intelligence)
- Segmented (e.g., Google: advertisers + users)
- Multi-sided (e.g., Uber: riders + drivers)
- Diversified (e.g., Amazon: consumers + AWS + advertisers)

**Questions**:
- Who are our most important customers?
- What are their jobs, pains, and gains?
- Are there distinct segments with different needs?

---

### 2. Value Proposition (What?)

**Define**: The products or services that create value for each segment.

**Value can be**:
- **Quantitative**: Price, speed, convenience
- **Qualitative**: Design, brand, reliability

**Examples**:
- **Uber**: "On-demand ride with cashless payment"
- **Slack**: "Reduces email by 30%"
- **Netflix**: "Unlimited streaming for $15/month"

**Questions**:
- What problem do we solve?
- What need do we satisfy?
- Why would a customer choose us over alternatives?

---

### 3. Channels (How?)

**Define**: How you reach and deliver value to customer segments.

**Phases**:
1. **Awareness**: How do customers find us? (ads, SEO, content, referrals)
2. **Evaluation**: How do they evaluate the offer? (trials, demos, reviews)
3. **Purchase**: How do they buy? (website, sales team, retail)
4. **Delivery**: How do we deliver value? (download, shipped, accessed)
5. **After-sales**: How do we support? (support, community, docs)

**Example** (Slack):
- **Awareness**: Content marketing, word of mouth
- **Evaluation**: Free trial (no credit card)
- **Purchase**: Self-serve subscription on website
- **Delivery**: SaaS (cloud-delivered)
- **After-sales**: Help center, community, support team

**Questions**:
- Which channels do our customers prefer?
- Which are most cost-effective?
- How do we integrate channels into a seamless experience?

---

### 4. Customer Relationships (What type?)

**Define**: How you interact with each segment.

**Types**:
- **Personal assistance**: Human interaction (chat, phone, email)
- **Self-service**: Fully automated (no human)
- **Communities**: User forums, social media
- **Co-creation**: Users contribute to product (reviews, content, code)
- **Dedicated personal assistance**: Account managers (enterprise)

**Example** (Amazon):
- **Personal**: Customer service phone/chat
- **Self-service**: 1-click ordering, automated recommendations
- **Community**: Reviews, Q&A
- **Dedicated**: AWS enterprise account managers

**Questions**:
- What relationship does each segment expect?
- How do we acquire, retain, and grow customers?
- What's the cost of maintaining these relationships?

---

### 5. Revenue Streams ($)

**Define**: How the business generates cash from each segment.

**Types**:
- **Asset sale**: Selling ownership of a product (e.g., Apple hardware)
- **Subscription**: Recurring fee for access (e.g., SaaS)
- **Usage fee**: Pay per use (e.g., AWS per-hour billing)
- **Leasing/Renting**: Temporary access (e.g., WeWork)
- **Licensing**: Fee for IP usage (e.g., Microsoft licensing)
- **Brokerage**: Fee for intermediation (e.g., Airbnb)
- **Advertising**: Fee for promotion (e.g., Google Ads)

**Questions**:
- What is each segment willing to pay?
- How do they prefer to pay?
- What are the margins on each stream?

---

### 6. Key Resources (What assets?)

**Define**: The assets required to deliver the value proposition.

**Types**:
- **Physical**: Factories, buildings, machines, vehicles
- **Intellectual**: Brand, patents, copyrights, data, partnerships
- **Human**: Engineers, salespeople, expertise
- **Financial**: Cash, lines of credit, investor capital

**Example** (Software company):
- **Intellectual**: Codebase, proprietary algorithms, brand
- **Human**: Engineering, product, sales teams
- **Financial**: VC funding, revenue

**Questions**:
- What resources does our value proposition require?
- Which are owned vs. leased?
- What's the cost of acquiring/maintaining them?

---

### 7. Key Activities (What work?)

**Define**: The most important actions needed to deliver the value proposition.

**Types**:
- **Production**: Manufacturing, assembling, creating
- **Problem-solving**: Consulting, engineering, services
- **Platform/network**: Running a marketplace, hosting a platform

**Example** (Airbnb):
- **Platform**: Maintaining the listing/search/booking platform
- **Trust & safety**: Verification, reviews, payments
- **Growth**: SEO, SEM, referral program

**Questions**:
- What activities does our value proposition require?
- Which are core vs. support?
- What's the cost or risk of each?

---

### 8. Key Partnerships (Who helps?)

**Define**: The network of suppliers and partners that make the model work.

**Motivations**:
- **Optimization**: Economy of scale (manufacturing partners)
- **Risk reduction**: Partnerships to share uncertainty (R&D consortiums)
- **Resource acquisition**: Access to skills, licenses, or customers

**Example** (Spotify):
- **Label partners**: Access to music catalog (value proposition)
- **Mobile carriers**: Bundled subscriptions (distribution)
- **Hardware partners**: Integration into cars, speakers

**Questions**:
- Who are our key partners and suppliers?
- What do we get from them (resources, activities, credibility)?
- What do they get from us?

---

### 9. Cost Structure ($)

**Define**: All costs incurred to operate the business model.

**Types**:
- **Fixed**: Rent, salaries, insurance (don't change with volume)
- **Variable**: COGS, payment fees, hosting (change with volume)
- **Economies of scale**: Cost per unit decreases as output increases
- **Economies of scope**: Cost advantage through breadth of operations

**Cost-driven vs. Value-driven**:
- **Cost-driven**: Minimize costs everywhere (e.g., budget airlines, IKEA)
- **Value-driven**: Premium on differentiation (e.g., luxury brands, Ritz-Carlton)

**Questions**:
- What are the most important costs?
- Which key resources and activities are most expensive?
- How do costs change with scale?

## Validation Questions

| Block | Validation Question |
|-------|---------------------|
| Customer Segments | Who is the actual end user? (not "everyone") |
| Value Proposition | Have you talked to 10 customers who say they'd pay? |
| Channels | What's the CAC by channel? |
| Customer Relationships | What's churn rate? |
| Revenue Streams | What's ARPU and LTV? |
| Key Resources | Can you build/acquire them within budget? |
| Key Activities | Which activities can you do vs. outsource? |
| Key Partnerships | Are terms documented in contracts? |
| Cost Structure | What's break-even? Burn rate? |

## Common Rationalizations

| Rationalization | Reality |
|-----------------|---------|
| "We have customers" | Do they pay? Do they come back? Are they the segment you think? |
| "The model is obvious" | If it's obvious, competitors copy it. Find your defensible advantage. |
| "One model fits all" | Each segment may need a different model. Multi-sided platforms are complex. |
| "We don't need partners" | You can't do everything. Partners reduce risk and accelerate growth. |

## Red Flags

- You can't name a specific customer segment (you say "everyone")
- Revenue streams are unproven (no one has paid yet)
- Cost structure > revenue (business is unsustainable)
- Key resources are unclear (what assets do you actually need?)
- No partners identified for critical capabilities

## Verification

- [ ] All 9 blocks filled out with specific, testable hypotheses
- [ ] Customer segments named (not "everyone")
- [ ] Value proposition tested with 10+ customer interviews
- [ ] Revenue streams identified and quantified (price, volume, margin)
- [ ] Cost structure estimated (break-even and burn rate)
- [ ] Key partnerships identified for critical gaps
- [ ] Assumptions prioritized and validated (highest risk first)
