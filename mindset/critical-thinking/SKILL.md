# Critical Thinking

Framework for analyzing arguments, identifying biases, evaluating evidence, and making sound logical conclusions.

## When to Use

- Evaluating business proposals or strategic decisions
- Analyzing research, data, or claims
- Debugging complex technical or operational problems
- Distinguishing good arguments from bad ones
- **When NOT to use**: Gut-feel or rapid decisions (use heuristics), or creative ideation (use brainstorming)

## Core Skills

### 1. Questioning Assumptions

**Examples of assumptions to question**:
- "We've always done it this way" (tradition bias)
- "Everyone does this" (bandwagon effect)
- "It seems obvious" (confirmation bias)
- "The data says X" (is the data reliable?)

**Tool**: "The Five Whys"
> Why is this the right approach? (Because of X) → Why is X true? (Because of Y) → Why is Y true? ... (peel until you hit bedrock)

---

### 2. Identifying Biases

| Bias | Definition | Mitigation |
|------|------------|------------|
| **Confirmation bias** | Seek evidence that confirms existing beliefs | Deliberately argue the opposite |
| **Anchoring** | Over-rely on first information received | Gather multiple perspectives before deciding |
| **Availability bias** | Judge likelihood by how easily examples come to mind | Seek base rates (actual frequencies) |
| **Survivorship bias** | Focus on successes, ignore failures | Study failures too |
| **Sunk cost fallacy** | Continue because of past investment | Ask: "Would I start this today?" |
| **Dunning-Kruger** | Overestimate ability in unfamiliar areas | Seek external validation, admit unknowns |

---

### 3. Evaluating Evidence

| Evidence Quality | Examples | Reliability |
|-----------------|----------|-------------|
| **Primary source** | Original research, raw data, firsthand account | Highest |
| **Peer-reviewed** | Published in reputable journal | High |
| **Expert opinion** | Domain authority with track record | Medium-High |
| **News/Media** | Journalistic reporting | Medium |
| **Anecdote** | "My friend said..." | Low |
| **Rumor** | Unverified, third-hand | Very Low |

**Checklist**:
- Is the source credible?
- Is the claim falsifiable?
- Is the sample size adequate?
- Was there a control group?
- Are conflicts of interest disclosed?

---

### 4. Logical Reasoning

**Common fallacies**:

| Fallacy | Example |
|---------|---------|
| **Ad hominem** | "Don't listen to them, they have no experience" |
| **Straw man** | "You want to cut all funding? That'll kill the team" (exaggeration) |
| **False dilemma** | "It's either this or total failure" |
| **Slippery slope** | "If we allow X, we'll end up with Z" |
| **Circular reasoning** | "It's true because it's true" |
| **Appeal to authority** | "The CEO says it, so it must be right" |
| **Correlation = causation** | "Ice cream sales cause drowning" |

**Tool**: "What's the evidence for and against?"
> List all arguments in favor, then all arguments against. Weight by quality, not count.

### 5. Systems Thinking (Interconnections)

See also: [systems-thinking](#)

- How do variables interact?
- What are the feedback loops?
- What are second-order effects?
- Who else is affected by this decision?

---

### 6. Decision-Making Under Uncertainty

**Tool**: Pre-mortem
> "Assume it's 12 months from now and our decision failed. What went wrong?"

**Tool**: Opportunity cost listing
> "If we do X, what are we NOT doing? Is that trade-off worth it?"

## Common Rationalizations

| Rationalization | Reality |
|-----------------|---------|
| "I'm too busy to question everything" | Critical thinking isn't for everything—it's for high-stakes decisions |
| "I'm naturally objective" | Everyone has biases. The question is whether you check them |
| "My gut has never steered me wrong" | Confirmation bias makes you remember wins and forget losses |
| "The experts agree" | Experts can be wrong (see: 2008 financial crisis, 2020 pandemic models) |

## Red Flags

- You can't articulate the counterargument to your position
- You rely on a single source for important information
- You feel threatened by contrary evidence
- You say "it's obvious" or "everyone knows"
- You find yourself using logical fallacies (or tolerating them in others)

## Verification

- [ ] Assumptions questioned: what am I assuming that might not be true?
- [ ] Cognitive biases considered: confirmation, anchoring, availability, etc.
- [ ] Evidence evaluated: source credibility, sample size, conflicts of interest
- [ ] Logical fallacies checked: no ad hominem, straw man, false dilemma, etc.
- [ ] Pre-mortem conducted: "what would failure look like and why?"
- [ ] Counterargument articulated: can you state the opposing view clearly?
